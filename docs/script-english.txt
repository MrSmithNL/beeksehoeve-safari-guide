Beeksehoove Safari Tour
Complete English Narration
A guided tour through the Veluwe wilderness
Chapter A: Welcome Aboard – The Sand Hares of the Heideweg
"Welcome aboard the safari wagon of the Beeksehoove! How wonderful that you're joining us on today's journey. We are a true family business and have been running the Beekse Hoeve with tremendous joy for over 20 years. For us, this is so much more than work; it's a passion for nature that got completely out of hand — and we love sharing it with you.
The Beeksehoove is located on the Heideweg. As the name suggests, this used to be the regular route along which shepherds drove their flocks of sheep and villagers walked to the vast heathlands. Our farm sits at a strategic spot: right on the threshold between the forest and the heath. So we've got one foot in the wilderness, yet we're only a five-minute walk from the charming centre of Otterlo. The perfect place for those who seek peace and quiet, but still want to pop out for an ice cream on foot!
You might be wondering: why the name Beeksehoove? Well, that's a real family affair. Our surname is Van Beek — which is a very handy name in this region, because our ancestors literally lived 'by a brook'. In Dutch, 'beek' means brook. And right behind our property, a real brook actually flows; which is why the neighbours named their campsite 'Beek en Hei' — Brook and Heath. The addition of 'Hoeve' is an old word for a farmstead large enough to feed an entire family. Don't worry — we no longer have to grow our own grain to survive the winter, but we proudly carry on the name.
Otterlo itself, by the way, is no newcomer. The village has existed for more than 1,170 years! It was first mentioned as far back as the year 855. The name actually means 'Forest on high sandy ground where otters live'. The otters have since retired or moved on, but we now have some 2,200 residents to take their place. The fact that we've become so popular isn't just thanks to nature, but also to our unique location right in the heart of the Netherlands. It's so central here that you can reach almost the entire country within an hour's drive. That combination of perfect accessibility and the vast Veluwe wilderness just around the corner — that really is something special.
In the old days, things were rather different. Life here was back-breaking toil on barren ground. The locals were even called 'sand hares'. Not because they had long ears, mind you, but because they were trying to survive in an enormous sandbox of shifting sands and heathland. Nowadays, we call that sandbox 'nature' and think it's absolutely magnificent! We're now leaving the inhabited world behind us. Buckle up — as far as we have any seatbelts — and enjoy the ride through the rugged wilderness of the Veluwe!"
Chapter B: The Koeweg – From Cattle Drive to E-bike
"We're now turning onto the Koeweg — the 'Cow Road'. The name was only officially recorded around the year 1900, but the road itself is of course much older. This used to be what's called a 'driftweg' — a drove road. These days, that might sound like something from a fast-car action movie, but it was actually the communal route for all the village livestock.
Back then, the farmers in Otterlo had very little land of their own near their farms. So the cows, sheep, goats and even the pigs had to be sent out onto the vast, open Veluwe every day to forage for their food. Each morning, all that livestock was driven — or 'drifted', as they used to say — down this road towards the open countryside. There were no fences back then, so the entire Veluwe was basically one enormous shared menu.
Whatever all those animals left behind on the way and in the barn was literally worth its weight in gold to the farmer. That manure was the only way to make the poor sandy soil of the fields fertile enough to grow crops. Without those 'cow pats' and droppings, the farmer simply wouldn't have had food on the table. So if you spot a pile of dung today: for the old Otterlo farmer, that was a small fortune!
Later, the road's function changed. It became an important trade route to the market in Ede. But the real transformation came with the rise of tourism. People no longer came to herd their cows, but arrived with the first motor cars and, of course, the famous 'caravan' to enjoy the peace and quiet. Nowadays, it's no longer cows drifting along this road, but mainly tourists on electric bikes making it their playground!
Today, we drive past several holiday parks and a popular off-lead dog area. The pigs and goats of yesteryear have been replaced by happy dogs and walkers. So you're travelling along a road with a rich history: from a manure route to a market route, to the road for your well-deserved day out on the Veluwe!"
Chapter C: The Border – Safe Jumpers & Clever Snouts
"We're now leaving the inhabited world of Otterlo and heading into the open countryside. We're driving along a charming, winding lane that leads us to the Planken Wambuis. Along the way, we pass a number of group holiday houses. It's a place of relaxation now, but it used to be quite different: these were once farmsteads where people worked incredibly hard. The cows and pigs of those days have gradually been swapped for cheerful groups of holidaymakers!
If you look outside, you'll see a low fence running along the road. This is the border between civilisation and the wilderness. Take a good look at the top of that fence. Can you see that strip of fine mesh? We call that 'dubbeltjesgaas' — 'dime mesh'. It's there especially for the roe deer and red deer. They're incredibly active animals, but in the world of nature we also call them 'lazy jumpers'.
That might sound odd, but it's all about their technique. They'd rather not make a free leap from a standing start; instead, they like to push off with their hind hooves against the top of the fence to flip themselves over. Without that fine mesh, their legs could get caught in the coarse wire netting, and we obviously want to prevent that. Thanks to the smooth dime mesh, they can push off safely and glide right over the edge. So it's basically a safety strip for our 'lazy jumpers', so they can keep enjoying nature without any injuries. The deer and stags, by the way, usually keep a respectful distance from the village; they're far too shy for that.
The wild boar, on the other hand... that's a completely different story. They're hardly stopped by anything and they actually love to raid the gardens in the village. For walkers, we have small swing gates, but the boar figured out that trick long ago. We regularly see 'father boar' rooting his powerful snout between the gate and simply pushing it open. He then holds the door, so that mother and the rest of the family can stroll in at their leisure. They are the boldest and perhaps the cleverest residents of the forest!
Also keep an eye out for the deep channels in the road. That's not poor maintenance, but our famous 'Veluwe gutters'. Here on the high sandy ground, we have no sewers. When there's a heavy downpour, all the water flows into these gullies, where it sinks straight through the sandy soil to replenish our natural groundwater reserves. A built-in water system courtesy of Mother Nature herself!"
Chapter D: The Hartenberg – A Place with a Big Heart
"We're now driving past the grounds of De Hartenberg, a unique location run by 's Heeren Loo. This isn't a holiday park, but a special care facility set up as a small, self-sufficient village. Here, people with intellectual disabilities live and work right in the heart of nature.
The name 'Hartenberg' has a lovely double meaning. In the past, this was an estate where many red deer — 'harten' in Dutch — lived on the hills. But nowadays, the name stands above all for the big heart of the people who live and work here. The wonderful thing about De Hartenberg is that it's an open community: as a passer-by, you're always welcome for a lovely walk around the grounds or a good cup of coffee at the teahouse.
There's all sorts to see here. The little village has its own church and a very popular petting farm. Many of the residents work here every day with great pride, whether that's in the grounds maintenance, with the animals, or in the catering. The focus here isn't on what someone can't do, but on what they can contribute.
Also take a look at the enormous rhododendrons along the road. Over the years, they've grown metres high and form a beautiful natural boundary. De Hartenberg is a place where tranquillity, care and hospitality come together. So if you have time later for a walk or a stop: the residents and the coffee are ready and waiting. And don't forget to give a wave — they absolutely love that here!"
Chapter E: Planken Wambuis – Coffins & Moss Huts
"We're about to officially enter the Planken Wambuis nature reserve. It's a striking name, but the meaning is actually a little peculiar. A 'Planken Wambuis' is an old Dutch word for a coffin — literally a 'plank jacket'.
The story goes that there was once an inn at the edge of this area that was so draughty and cold that guests would joke: 'I wouldn't want to be found dead in a plank jacket in here!' Another explanation is that the inn was so remote that it was the last stop for travellers crossing the dangerous, vast heathland. Those who didn't survive the journey across the wild moors unfortunately ended up in just such a wooden jacket. A reassuring thought: today's route is considerably more comfortable!
We'll shortly be driving across these sweeping fields towards De Mossel. Especially in the 19th century, this area looked very different: it was one enormous, open heath where tens of thousands of sheep grazed for their manure. The shepherds would spend the night in simple 'moss huts'. These were small turf shelters, half-dug into the ground and covered with moss and sods to keep the biting wind out. It was a lonely and gruelling existence in all weathers; a world away from how we experience the Veluwe today.
We're now at the lowest point of the Valenberg; from here, it's uphill all the way. As we climb, I'd ask you to keep your cameras at the ready and lower your voices just a touch. We are guests here in the 'living room' of the wildlife. With a bit of luck, we'll soon be standing face to face with the residents of this area."
Chapter F: The Wild Boar Special – Rooting & Wellness
"Take a good look at the verges along the cycle path. It looks as though an army of diggers came through last night, but this is the handiwork of the wild boar. They have a tremendously powerful nose, but their eyes rather let them down. A wild boar is what we call 'tunnel-blind'.
They're stuck in a kind of tunnel vision: as long as that nose is buried in the ground rooting for beetles and larvae, they see almost nothing of their surroundings. But don't let that fool you! Although they sometimes look a bit clumsy, they can reach enormous speeds out of nowhere. A sprinting boar is a real powerhouse on trotters. Especially when piglets — the famous 'frischlings' — are nearby, mother boar is extremely vigilant. So always keep a good distance. They fiercely protect their young, and you absolutely do not want a confrontation!
Why do they love rooting here along the cycle path? Pure convenience. The path is warmed by the sun during the day, and that warmth attracts insects and larvae that burrow under the edge of the path. The boar smells that 'warm buffet' and digs the treats right out from under the path's edge.
But a boar also loves a bit of wellness. A muddy hollow in the field is what we call a 'wallow'. This mud bath is an absolute necessity: the mud suffocates ticks and lice in their coat. After the bath comes the aftercare at the 'rubbing trees'. Can you see those bare, smooth patches at the base of the trunks? That's where the boar presses its full weight against the bark to scrape off dirt and parasites. The dried mud that stays on acts as a natural sunscreen and protects them from insect bites.
You'll often smell them before you see them, by the way; they give off a very distinctive, strong scent rather like Maggi seasoning. So: nose to the wind and camera at the ready, but remember to keep that safe distance!"
Chapter G: The Burial Mounds – The Princes of the Veluwe
"If you look carefully around you in this rolling landscape, you'll spot here and there some striking, round hills that look just a little too perfectly shaped to be natural. And you'd be right: these aren't ordinary hills, but burial mounds. You're looking directly at prehistoric graves more than 4,000 years old.
In the municipality of Ede alone, there are approximately a hundred of these monuments. It's extraordinary to think that here, long before there were sat-navs or paved roads, people already lived who honoured their dead with great respect. In the oldest mounds, the most important people of that era were often buried in a curled-up position, together with their most precious possessions for the afterlife, such as the famous Veluwe Bell Beakers. Later, in the Bronze Age, the ritual changed and the ashes of the deceased were placed in urns within these mounds.
These burial mounds were very carefully positioned. They were placed on high, dry spots along the most important routes. Because the Veluwe was once a bare, treeless heath, these mounds could be seen from kilometres away. They were the landmarks and waypoints for travellers thousands of years ago; a dignified sign that one was entering an inhabited area.
Today, they're protected as archaeological monuments. We'll leave the peace of these 'Princes of the Veluwe' undisturbed, as we pass through this age-old landscape at a respectful distance."
Chapter H: The Avenues of Doorwerth – Hunting & Nobility
"Just imagine: let's go back in time for a moment. Forget this wagon; we're sitting in a rattling horse-drawn carriage, wrapped in heavy coats, with a hunting rifle across our knee. We're driving here across the private grounds of the barons and knights of Doorwerth Castle. Although their castle lies about 15 kilometres away as the crow flies, this was their own gigantic 'back garden'.
Look at those dead-straight avenues of oaks and beeches. The nobility did enjoy making an impression; they had these paths laid out as straight as a royal entrance for their hunting parties. These trees were their natural treasure chest. The thick trunks were destined for construction, and the pruned branches were used to make boundary fences. Buying fences was impossibly expensive for such enormous areas in those days, so they used what the forest offered to mark the boundaries. Whatever was left went straight to the castle to heat the ice-cold halls.
In those days, this area was absolutely forbidden territory for ordinary folk. Anyone found wandering here uninvited could expect rather rough treatment from the gamekeepers. Only 'high society' was allowed to hunt the boar and red deer here.
Fortunately, the land was later sold to the state and the peace has returned for everyone. The avenues of the barons now work in our favour: those long, open sight lines between the trunks are the perfect windows for spotting the wildlife of the Veluwe. We can still taste the atmosphere of days gone by here, but without having to fear the wrath of the baron!"
Chapter I: The Valenberg – The Eye of the Veluwe
"We're now approaching the Valenberg, one of the highest points in this area. Because of its strategic position and the sweeping views over the Mosselse Veld, this was once a crucial spot for fire watch duty.
In the past, a tall wooden fire tower stood here, which was known as the 'Eye of the Veluwe'. Forest rangers kept watch over the horizon day and night. In an era when the heath was still bare and vast, they could see for kilometres from this point. Every wisp of smoke was spotted immediately, helping to prevent major wildfires.
That old tower has since been retired, but we've gained something wonderful in its place: a modern viewing platform that's accessible to everyone. Via a sturdy ramp, you can walk right up to the panorama deck. At the top, you'll find an orientation table that tells you exactly what you're looking at. On a clear day, you can see the outline of Arnhem and even spot the transmission mast at Ugchelen. It's basically Google Maps, but in real life and with much more fresh air!
Also keep a keen eye on the forest edges and the valley below us. Besides the ponies and the Spanish cattle, this is an excellent spot to see a roe deer or other wildlife darting away. With a bit of luck and a sharp pair of eyes, you can spot them here with the naked eye. It's a place where the vastness of the Veluwe is truly palpable; a moment of calm and space on the rooftop of the region."
Chapter J: De Mossel – An Oasis in the Wilderness
"We're now driving through the De Mossel nature area, which used to belong entirely to the farm. The history of this place goes way back; the name 'Mosschel' was already recorded around the year 1500. It refers to the simple turf huts of the shepherds. Unfortunately for the foodies among us: you won't find any mussels here — despite what the name might suggest — but you will find delicious apple pie and coffee at the teahouse we'll be passing shortly. But... we don't always stop there, so you'll just have to make do with the aroma for now!
The farm used to serve a double purpose. Besides farming, this was a vital resting place where travellers could safely eat, sleep and stable their horse before continuing their journey across the Veluwe. Since 1938, the farm has been home to the Prangsma family. During the Second World War, this remote spot even played a heroic role by sheltering no fewer than 53 people in hiding.
For decades, people toiled here with horse and plough to grow rye, oats and maize. Since 1990, farming has stopped and nature has reclaimed the land, but the tradition of the resting place lives on! Where the working horses once stood, it's now a hub for horse riders and carriage drivers exploring the beautiful routes through this area.
Around the farm lies the true wilderness. In the vast quiet zones, red deer, wild boar, roe deer and foxes live in total freedom. And keep your eyes peeled: this is nowadays also the official territory of the wolf. You'll see information signs here and there, but don't worry: you're not on his menu. The wolf has excellent taste and prefers a wild boar or a roe deer over a tourist. And let's be honest: with all that outdoor clothing and hiking boots these days, you'd be far too tough to eat! As long as we give him space and don't invite him for a selfie, he'll leave us in peace too."
Chapter K: Across the Terrace of De Mossel
"Look around you now for a unique moment: where else would you find a safari wagon literally driving across the terrace of a restaurant? Well... if it's summer, that is — otherwise we're mainly weaving between scarves and woolly hats! As we inch our way between the coffee cups, you can see on your side the old farmhouse from 1863 and the new sheep barn.
The history of the Prangsma family begins here in 1938, when Grandpa Prangsma came to live here to cultivate the land. Grandma followed a few years later and started the tearoom after the war. In those days, they rented out the farmhouse in summer to boarding guests, for whom Grandma cooked three meals a day. In 1973, Piet and Valentine took over, and since 2011, the third generation — Chantal and Danny — have been at the helm.
You can also see the beautiful new sheep barn here. The old, characterful barn was sadly destroyed by a huge fire in 2016, but in 2019 this new building rose in its place. It's now a modern accommodation with lots of glass, so that even indoors you're still right in the middle of nature. Grandma's old 'boarding kitchen' has been converted into a cosy apartment. Besides the sheep that sometimes spend the night here — when the 'real' barn is just a bit too far to walk — guests can also still stay overnight in the group accommodation or the shepherd's wagon.
Look at the hustle and bustle on the terrace; it's a wonderful contrast with the solitude of the heath we've just crossed. Feel free to give a wave to the guests enjoying their coffee and apple pie here — though be warned, they usually look at our wagon with just a touch of healthy envy!
In winter, the wood stove crackles inside and in summer it's lovely to sit outside. In partnership with this unique spot, we also offer fun packages, such as a delicious lunch or sharing platter combined with the Safari Express. In winter, they serve hot soup and mulled wine. Can you picture yourself here already?"
Chapter L: The Stone Sculptures of Adri Verhoeven
"In the middle of this wilderness, you suddenly spot something you might not quite expect here: gleaming stones rising from the sand. This is the artwork 'Kudde' — meaning 'Herd' — created in 2012 by artist Adri Verhoeven. It's part of the Ede Stone Sculpture Route, an impressive series of twelve artworks that starts in the centre of Ede and runs all the way to the Hoge Veluwe National Park. With this, we attract a whole new audience to the area: from nature lovers to true art connoisseurs.
The artist used enormous erratics of grey and red granite for this work. It was a massive operation to get these giants to this remote location. These are stones that, just like the hills we've been driving over, were pushed here from Scandinavia by glacial ice during the Ice Age. So they've been lying here for thousands of years, although we're not sure whether they were always in this exact spot.
Adri Verhoeven worked these millions-of-years-old stones and polished parts of them to a mirror finish, but so skilfully that you can't see any trace of human tools. Because real herds of horses and cattle often graze in this field, he gave the artwork the fitting name 'Herd'. The great advantage of this artificial herd? You never have to feed them and they never wander off!
The effect is stunning: the polished surfaces catch the light and colours of the landscape, making the stones almost seem to breathe. They populate the open field, nestle against each other or cluster with their kind beneath a tree. It's a remarkable contrast between the rough, weathered 'skin' of the stone and the smooth side that literally reveals its inner self to the world. According to Adri.
Take a good look as we pass; when the sun hits them, they reflect the beauty of the heath in their own very special way."
Chapter M: The Great Wilderness Guide – Meet the Residents
"To give you a good picture of who's actually in charge here, I've lined up the residents of the Planken Wambuis for you. We make a distinction between the 'managers' that have been introduced here and the 'true wildlife' that lives here in complete freedom.
The Large Grazers: The Landscape Managers
Natuurmonumenten — the Dutch nature conservation organisation — uses large grazers here to prevent these beautiful plains from turning into one vast, dense forest.
The Sayaguesa cattle: If you see them standing there, impressive and jet-black, you're actually looking at the spitting image of the extinct aurochs. The bulls are enormous and can weigh up to 1,000 kilograms. With their long, impressive horns, they keep the wilderness in check by eating grasses, herbs and even young tree shoots.
The New Forest ponies: These horses are the tough ones of the area. They're medium-sized, very sturdy ponies that you'll see in all sorts of colours: from brown to grey. They weigh on average between 250 and 400 kilograms. Their secret? They don't just eat grass, but also munch through the tough heather plants, which is essential for preserving this landscape.
The 'Big Five' of the Veluwe
Now for the animals that most of you came here for today. Our very own wild Big Five:
The Red Deer: This is the undisputed king of the Netherlands. A fully grown stag is our largest land mammal and weighs up to 200 kilograms. In summer, you'll see them in a reddish-brown coat, but in winter they turn grey-brown. The males carry an impressive set of antlers. They eat grasses and heather, but don't hesitate to strip bark, buds and acorns from the menu.
The Wild Boar: The real 'rooters'. An adult male, also called a boar, often weighs between 80 and 150 kilograms. They have a dark brown to black, coarse coat and that distinctive rooting snout. The piglets are world-famous for their striped 'pyjamas'. They're omnivores: roots, insects, acorns and even small rodents all disappear into their stomach.
The Roe Deer: Much smaller and more elegant than the red deer. A roe deer weighs only 20 to 30 kilograms. They're sandy yellow to reddish-brown and you'll recognise them by the white patch on their rump — the 'mirror'. They're real gourmets; they specifically seek out brambles, young leaves, berries and twigs.
The Mouflon: A magnificent wild sheep. The rams have those huge, characteristic curled horns and often a white 'saddle patch' on the back. They weigh between 35 and 50 kilograms. Although they're indispensable for keeping the heath open, here's an important fact: they only roam behind the fences of the Hoge Veluwe National Park, right nearby.
The Wolf: The most mysterious resident. Weighing 35 to 45 kilograms, he resembles a large shepherd dog, but his broader head, shorter ears and the tail he often carries low make him unique. He's a pure carnivore who hunts the roe deer, mouflons and young wild boar here.
The Hidden Treasures
Look beyond the obvious, because this area is rich in so much more. In the sky, you'll see the Nightjar, the Hobby and the extremely rare Honey Buzzard. On the sunny sandy patches we're passing now, you might spot the Sand Lizard or the legless lizard, the Slow Worm, basking in the sun. And deep from the cover of the bushes, the Pine Marten and the Fox have probably been watching us the whole time.
Keep your binoculars at the ready, because at the Planken Wambuis you never know who's grazing around the next corner!"
Chapter N: Fire, Management and Wildlife Trails
"If we look around us, you'll see that the forest keeps changing its face. Those neat rows of trees? They're the old production forests from the early 20th century. Back then, they were intended for the mining industry, because Scots pine makes a loud 'singing' — or creaking — sound before it breaks. That was a life-saving signal for the miners. Nowadays, those trees sing a different tune: they end up as your new dining table or the window frames in a sustainable housing estate. And don't worry about the felling: fortunately, the trees here grow enormously fast, so on balance we lose nothing at all!
But in this beautiful forest, a danger also lurks: a major wildfire.
Look at the wide sandy paths we're driving on now. These are important paths: the firebreaks. In dry summers, the Veluwe turns bone-dry. A single spark from a cigar or a forgotten glass bottle in the sun can be enough to start an enormous blaze. These paths have been designed to be wide enough to stop the flames. If a fire breaks out, extra trees are immediately felled so the fire can't jump across. At the same time, these paths serve as roads for the fire brigade, so they can reach any spot quickly.
Sometimes the land managers deliberately set fire to the heather themselves: controlled burning. It's exciting precision work; we burn off the old, woody heather so that the seeds can germinate on the blackened ground. Why? Because old heather tastes like a three-year-old dry biscuit. But those young, fresh shoots that spring up after the fire? That's the real 'delicacy' of the Veluwe for our cattle and ponies. What's more, this young heather is stunning to look at; by the end of August, everything here is in glorious bloom again.
While we bump along these wide sandy tracks, the animals have their own secret road network: the wildlife trails. If you look closely, you'll see narrow, beaten paths between the bushes. Those are the motorways of the deer, roe deer and boar. Without any sat-nav, they know exactly how to get from their bedroom to the 'pub' — the drinking pool!"
Chapter O: The Guardians of the Forest
"You might be wondering who manages all this beauty. At the Planken Wambuis, that's Natuurmonumenten, while other parts of the Veluwe fall under Staatsbosbeheer or the Hoge Veluwe National Park foundation.
These organisations are the guardians of our nature. They ensure the balance: on one hand, the protection of rare plants and animals, and on the other, the opportunity for us to enjoy it all. That's an ongoing puzzle. They have to decide where the grazers may roam and which paths stay open to the public.
The wonderful thing is that these organisations can only do this thanks to the support of their members and donors. Without them, we'd see the heath turn to grass and the historic burial mounds disappear under moss. If you've enjoyed this unique environment today, perhaps consider supporting these guardians. That way, together, we ensure that this piece of ancient Netherlands is preserved for the generations to come!" For more information, visit the Natuurmonumenten website.
Chapter Closing: The Return to Otterlo – Back to the Sand Hares
"Our safari is unfortunately coming to an end. We're leaving the Planken Wambuis and De Mossel behind and slowly driving back into Otterlo. Take one last good look around you; you'll notice that you now see the landscape with completely different eyes.
Don't forget: this area is different every season. In winter, we ride cosily with the heating on, and in summer the canopies come off for the fresh forest air. You're now a true expert: you know why those channels are in the road and why the boar love to root along the cycle paths. And you now also know that the deer, thanks to the clever dime mesh, only jump in the right places so they always land safely on the other side.
You now also know what a 'Planken Wambuis' actually is and that we don't find mussels here, but rather the 'Hut of Moss'. We hope you've enjoyed the stories and the nature just as much as we do every single day; this truly remains a special place!
But did you know that our Safari Express also operates on location? We provide bespoke services for weddings, transport from A to B, or even a trip that starts from your own home; the possibilities are enormous. Have a look at the Beeksehoove website. Besides the safari tours, we also offer a unique B&B and exciting activities such as raft building, team building or bamboo construction.
On behalf of the team at the Beeksehoove, we'd like to thank you warmly for coming. It's an honour for us to have been able to show you this beautiful piece of the Veluwe. Whether you're going to linger in the village, sit down at a cosy restaurant, or start your journey home: we wish you a safe trip and hopefully see you next time!
Don't forget to take all your belongings from the wagon and... do spread the word, but keep the very best spots we've shown you just a little bit secret for yourself!
Thank you, from the Beeksehoove!"
